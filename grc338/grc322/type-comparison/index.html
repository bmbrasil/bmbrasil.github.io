DIN vs. Interstate

History of each typeface

DIN

DIN began as a German standard lettering system used for technical, transportation, and public information. It was developed under the Deutsches Institut für Normung, or German Institute for Standardization, and was officially published as DIN 1451 in the 1930s. The design was connected to earlier Prussian railway lettering and was later used on German traffic signs, engineering drawings, technical documents, and public signage. Its purpose was not decoration, but consistency, efficiency, and easy reproduction. Because of its simple geometric shapes and industrial clarity, DIN later became popular in graphic design, branding, and editorial design.

Interstate

Interstate was designed by Tobias Frere-Jones and released by Font Bureau in the 1990s. It was inspired by Highway Gothic, the lettering system used for United States highway signs. Highway Gothic was designed for the Federal Highway Administration in the late 1940s so drivers could read signs clearly from a distance and at high speed. Frere-Jones adapted those practical sign forms into a more complete and flexible type family. Interstate keeps the direct, public-information feeling of highway signage, but its wider spacing, open counters, and distinctive details make it useful for branding, publications, packaging, and digital design.
